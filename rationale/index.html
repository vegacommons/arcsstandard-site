Why Agentic Systems Need Record Governance

ARCS — Agentic Record Custody Standard

Vega Commons Project  |  March 2026

AI agent systems are not only inference systems or action systems. They are record-generating systems. Every agent interaction produces artifacts: prompts, responses, planning traces, tool-call logs, memory updates, intermediate reasoning, and derived outputs. These records are created as a condition of system operation, not as deliberate publication.

Existing standards for agentic systems address communication, identity, and behavioral control. Protocols such as the Model Context Protocol (MCP), Agent-to-Agent Protocol (A2A), and Agent Communication Protocol (ACP) define how agents exchange messages, delegate tasks, and access tools. Guardrail systems define what agents are permitted to do. Infrastructure controls define where execution occurs.

None of these define how the records produced by agentic execution are classified, assigned, propagated, retained, or discarded.

Why the Gap Matters

This gap becomes significant as agentic systems are deployed in environments where records carry legal, regulatory, or operational consequences. Financial institutions, insurers, pharmaceutical companies, government contractors, and litigation-exposed enterprises must account for the records generated by autonomous systems. Without a consistent model, organizations cannot reliably determine which records exist, where they reside, which system has custody, or what retention obligations apply.

What Makes Agentic Records Different

Four characteristics of agentic execution make traditional governance models insufficient.

Records may be generated without explicit user intent. An agent may create intermediate state, planning traces, or memory artifacts that persist beyond the immediate interaction. These records may not be visible at the interface level, but may still exist within underlying systems.

Records may propagate across multiple vendors during a single workflow. A task initiated in one system may result in calls to external tools, model providers, or other agents. Each step may create records subject to different retention rules, access controls, or disclosure obligations.

Records may exist simultaneously on multiple custody surfaces. A single interaction may produce artifacts stored in a platform database, a model provider log, and a productivity integration. Without a defined model of custody assignment, it is unclear which surface is authoritative or which policy governs the record.

Agentic systems may generate new classes of artifacts that do not fit existing categories. Deliberative traces, reflection memory, planning state, and multi-agent conference outputs may not be easily classified as user content, system logs, or application data, yet they may still carry operational or legal significance.

What ARCS Does

The Agentic Record Custody Standard (ARCS) defines a governance model for records produced by agentic systems. The standard addresses four governance objects:

  1. Classification. Records are assigned to defined classes that determine their governance posture. Classification occurs before retention or propagation decisions are made.
  2. Custody assignment. Each record is associated with a custody surface identifying the system responsible for storage and policy enforcement. Custody may exist on more than one surface simultaneously.
  3. Retention posture. Each record carries a declared posture—ephemeral, session-bounded, governed-persistent, or durable—that determines the expected lifecycle of the record and the obligations attached to it.
  4. Propagation. When records move across systems, governance information travels with them. Classification, custody, and retention posture remain interpretable after the record crosses vendor, platform, or runtime boundaries.

ARCS does not prescribe how agents are built, how models are selected, or how workflows are executed. It defines the conditions under which records created by those workflows can be understood and governed consistently.

Why Now

As agentic systems become more autonomous, more distributed, and more persistent, the absence of a record governance model becomes a structural risk. Independent platforms may implement incompatible retention rules, undefined propagation behavior, or opaque storage practices. Once such patterns become widespread, retroactive standardization becomes difficult.

Defining the model early allows implementations to converge on compatible assumptions before fragmentation occurs.
